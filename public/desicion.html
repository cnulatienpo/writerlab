⚖️ Decision

A decision is when the character chooses—actively, knowingly, and with consequence. It defines them. It moves the story. And it shows us who they are when it counts.

📘 Lesson

It’s not about what happens to them. It’s what they choose to do. Good decisions, bad ones, desperate ones—they all carry weight. The story should change because of what they decide.

📚 Examples

Thelma & Louise – They drive off the cliff. No hesitation, no turning back.
Breaking Bad – Walter chooses not to take the money. He goes back to the game.
Inside Out – Joy hands the controls to Sadness. That’s the real growth.

🧩 Tropes: Forces of Choice

The Ultimatum – They must choose now or lose everything.
The Moral Fork – Right vs easy. They can’t have both.
The Clock Is Ticking – Time makes the decision urgent.
The Point of No Return – Once chosen, they can’t go back.
The Lesser Evil – Both options hurt.
Choose One Life – Save one, lose another.
The Big Reveal – New truth demands a new decision.
The Temptation – What they want is right there—will they take it?
The Past Returns – A choice they made before resurfaces now.
The Hero’s Reluctance – They don’t want to decide, but they have to.
Cross the Line – Will they do something they swore they never would?
Take the Throne – Step into power… or reject it.
Abandon the Mission – Loyalty or freedom?
The Vow Breaker – They promised. Now it costs them.
The Sacrifice – Someone has to lose.
The Confession – Truth or comfort?
The Door Closes – It’s now or never.
Love or Duty – Can’t have both. Choose.
The Redemption Offer – One shot to change who they are.
Change Sides – Stay loyal… or join the enemy.

⛔ Tropes: Choice Avoidance

The Passive Protagonist – Things just happen to them.
The Deus Ex Solution – Magic or luck makes the decision go away.
The Endless Delay – They keep stalling the choice.
The Flinch – They start to act… then back out.
The Offscreen Decision – We don’t see them choose—we’re just told.
The Non-Decision – They agree with whatever’s happening.
The It-Was-Obvious – The choice is too easy.
The Coin Toss – They don’t decide. They leave it to chance.
The Group Vote – The protagonist avoids choosing alone.
The Silent Walk-Away – They ghost the problem instead of resolving it.
The Collapse – Emotional overload stops them from acting.
The Outsider’s Call – Someone else chooses for them.
The Win-Win Copout – They get both options with no cost.
The Monologue Stall – They talk instead of act.
The Sleep-On-It – They literally avoid deciding until later.
The Distraction Detour – Something interrupts the moment.
The Ignored Call – They refuse to face the choice.
The Endless Pro/Con List – Indecision paralyzed by thought.
The Scene Skip – Story moves on without showing the decision.
The Always Agreeable – They never push back.
